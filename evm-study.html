Foundation values
PV
Planned Value
% planned × BAC

What you planned to have done by now, expressed in dollars. The budget baseline at a point in time. If you planned 50% done and BAC = $100k, PV = $50k.
EV
Earned Value
% complete × BAC

The value of work actually done. You "earn" it by completing work — not by spending money. EV is the anchor of every other formula.
AC
Actual Cost
given in scenario
tap to expand
What you actually spent. Always given — never calculated from other values. If the question gives you AC, use it directly.
BAC
Budget at Completion
total approved budget
tap to expand
The original total budget for all project work. Fixed unless a formal change request is approved through change control.
Variances — negative is always bad
Schedule & cost variance
CV
Cost Variance
EV − AC
tap to expand
Are we over or under budget? Negative = overspent. Memory: "Did we earn more than we spent?" EV − AC. If you earned $80k but spent $90k, CV = −$10k.
SV
Schedule Variance
EV − PV
tap to expand
Are we ahead or behind schedule? Negative = behind. Memory: "Did we earn what we planned to earn?" Note: SV trends toward zero at project end.
Performance indexes — 1.0 is perfect, above 1.0 is good
Efficiency ratios
CPI
Cost Performance Index
EV ÷ AC
tap to expand
The most important EVM metric. CPI 0.8 = getting $0.80 of value per $1 spent. Below 1.0 = over budget. Research shows CPI rarely improves significantly after 20% complete.
SPI
Schedule Performance Index
EV ÷ PV
tap to expand
Below 1.0 = behind schedule. SPI is less reliable than CPI late in the project because it converges toward 1.0 as all work gets done.
Forecasts — where are we headed?
Completion estimates
EAC₁
Estimate at Completion (typical)
BAC ÷ CPI
tap to expand
Use when current CPI efficiency will continue for the rest of the project. Most common on the exam. "If we keep performing this way..."
EAC₂
EAC — fresh re-estimate
AC + ETC
tap to expand
Use when the original estimate is fundamentally flawed and the team provides a new bottom-up estimate for remaining work (ETC).
EAC₃
EAC — both CPI & SPI
AC + [(BAC−EV) ÷ (CPI×SPI)]
tap to expand
Use when both cost and schedule efficiency affect remaining work. Less common but appears on harder questions involving tight deadlines.
ETC
Estimate to Complete
EAC − AC
tap to expand
How much more money is needed? Simple: EAC minus what you've already spent. Or given directly as a fresh bottom-up estimate from the team.
VAC
Variance at Completion
BAC − EAC
tap to expand
How over or under budget will the project finish? Negative = projected to finish over budget. This is the bottom-line forecast for the sponsor.
TCPI
To-Complete Performance Index
(BAC−EV) ÷ (BAC−AC)
tap to expand
Efficiency required on remaining work to hit BAC. TCPI > 1.1 is generally considered unrealistic — recommend revising EAC instead. TCPI > 1.0 means you need to work harder than current pace.

Memory anchor
The EV formula pattern

Almost every EVM formula either starts with EV or divides something by EV.

Variances → EV minus something (EV−AC, EV−PV)
Indexes → EV divided by something (EV÷AC, EV÷PV)
Forecasts → work backward from BAC using CPI

If you lose your place in a calculation, find EV first. Everything else follows.

Sunk cost trap

PMI never accepts "continue because we've already spent so much."

If a project has poor CPI and TCPI > 1.1, the correct response is to surface this to the sponsor and recommend a formal decision — not to push harder. EVM gives you the data; governance makes the call. A PM who hides bad EVM data to avoid sponsor discomfort is exhibiting the wrong behavior.

Exam signal decoder
"Assuming current trends continue" → BAC ÷ CPI
"The team re-estimated the remaining work as $X" → AC + ETC (ETC = that $X)
"The original estimate is no longer valid" → AC + ETC
"Both CPI and SPI should be considered" → AC + [(BAC−EV) ÷ (CPI×SPI)]
No qualifier given → default to BAC ÷ CPI
